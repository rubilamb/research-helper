# Anderson (2022) Introduction Quality Checklist

Source: Anderson, A.J. (2022). Writing an Introduction to a scientific paper.
Ophthalmic and Physiological Optics, 43(1), 1-3. DOI: 10.1111/opo.13071

Run this checklist INTERNALLY after drafting an introduction. Do NOT display
the checklist in the output unless the user explicitly asks for it.
Revise the draft until all indicators pass, then deliver the clean version.

---

## Indicator 1: When was it written?

PASS: The introduction (or at minimum a bulleted logical path) was written
BEFORE the study was designed and conducted.

FAIL: The introduction was written after the study was completed, with no
prior scaffold.

Why it matters: Writing the introduction last leads to HARKing
(Hypothesizing After Results are Known; Kerr, 1998). Authors who write
the introduction last frequently discover relevant prior studies only after
completing their work, leading to undisclosed replications.

Scoring note: For pre-study outputs from this skill, this indicator
automatically passes because the placeholder framework IS the pre-study scaffold.

---

## Indicator 2: Is there a hypothesis?

PASS: The introduction contains an explicit hypothesis or clearly stated
research question / aim.

FAIL: There is no hypothesis. The paper describes what was done without
stating what was expected or what question was being answered.

Why it matters: A hypothesis-free introduction suggests the study was
exploratory without a logical basis, OR that the hypothesis was manufactured
post-hoc.

Quick fix: Add to Move 3: "We hypothesise that..." or
"The research question is: does X cause Y?"

---

## Indicator 3: Can a reader predict the experiment?

PASS: A naive reader who finishes reading Move 2 (with Move 3 removed)
can accurately predict what experiment the paper is about to describe.

FAIL: The logical path through Move 1 and Move 2 does not lead to a
predictable experiment. The reader is surprised by Move 3.

The Magic Trick Test: Give the introduction (minus Move 3) to someone
unfamiliar with the paper. Ask: "What study do you think is about to
be described?"

Quick fix: Make the gap statement more directed. Instead of "no study has
examined X", write "no study has examined whether X causes Y, which would
explain why previous findings conflict."

---

## Indicator 4: Are conjunctions present?

PASS: The introduction uses linking conjunctions that make the logical
structure explicit:
- "However..." (conflict or limitation)
- "Therefore...", "Hence...", "As such..." (logical extension)
- "Because of this...", "Consequently..." (causal link)
- "Despite this...", "Nevertheless..." (concession then pivot)

FAIL: Conjunctions are largely absent. The introduction reads as a series
of disconnected factual statements.

The deletion test: Remove all sentences that cite prior studies. Is the
introduction still readable? If YES, it is a list, not a logical argument.

Quick fix: Review every sentence transition. Add However / Therefore /
As such / Hence where logical relationships exist.

---

## Indicator 5: Citation traceability

PASS: Every (Author, Year) citation in the introduction maps to a paper
in the reference table. No citation is invented or unverifiable.

FAIL: The introduction contains citations that cannot be traced to a real,
retrieved paper.

Why it matters: Fabricated citations are the single biggest risk in
AI-assisted academic writing. This indicator catches hallucinated references.

Quick fix: Cross-check every citation against the reference table.
Replace any unverifiable citation with [CITATION NEEDED].

---

## Scoring Summary

5/5: Introduction is structurally sound. Deliver to user.
4/5: Identify which indicator fails, apply the quick fix, re-score.
3/5 or below: Significant revision needed before delivery.

Priority order:
- Indicators 2 and 5 are most critical (hypothesis + real citations)
- Indicator 3 tests logical coherence
- Indicator 4 is most easily fixable (add conjunctions)
- Indicator 1 is contextual (pre-study vs. post-study)
