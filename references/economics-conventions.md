# Economics Research Writing Conventions
# Load when user's field is economics, finance, or quantitative social science.

---

## Economics-Specific Move 1 Conventions

### Standard opening pattern
1. State the economic phenomenon and its importance
   ("Energy price volatility has significant implications for investment
   decisions, fiscal policy, and household welfare...")
2. Connect to the broader literature strand
   ("A large body of empirical work has examined the determinants of
   commodity price dynamics...")
3. Review relevant methodological approaches
   ("Recent advances in machine learning have been applied to forecasting
   problems in financial markets...")

### Contribution positioning types (UCLA Writing Center, n.d.)
- Inquisitive: "An interesting correlation exists between X and Y that
  prior work has not exploited..."
- Paradoxical: "Despite the consensus that X causes Y, the mechanism
  remains disputed..."
- Corrective: "Despite extensive focus on X, the role of Y has been
  overlooked..."

### Tense rules (Gray, 2018)
- General truths about markets or phenomena: simple present
  ("Energy prices exhibit high volatility...")
- General body of literature: present perfect
  ("Studies have documented a relationship between X and Y...")
- Specific paper results: simple past
  ("Brunetti and Lildholdt (2007) showed that...")
- Your own study's aims: simple past or simple present
  ("This paper examines...", "We estimate...")

## Economics-Specific Gap Types

1. Data gap: new or higher-frequency data that prior work did not have
   ("Prior studies relied on monthly data; daily data from [source]
   enables finer-grained identification of...")

2. Method gap: new identification strategy or approach not previously applied
   ("Existing models rely on linear specifications; non-linear approaches
   may substantially improve...")

3. Context gap: phenomenon studied elsewhere but not in your setting
   ("While extensive evidence exists for US markets, the European
   context has received less attention...")

4. Period gap: structural break or recent shock not yet covered
   ("Prior literature predates the 2021-2022 energy crisis; whether
   existing relationships hold under extreme conditions is unknown...")

5. Mechanism gap: effect documented but transmission unclear
   ("The effect has been documented, but the mechanism through which it
   propagates remains unclear...")

6. Conflict gap: two bodies of work disagree
   ("Studies disagree on whether X causes Y. A plausible explanation is
   [hypothesis]. No study has directly tested this.")

### Gap statement phrases
- "However, the literature has largely ignored..."
- "Despite this, no study has examined whether..."
- "The relationship between X and Y in [context] remains poorly understood"
- "Existing models fail to account for..."
- "The evidence on X is limited to [context/period], leaving open the
  question of whether..."

## Economics-Specific Move 3 Conventions

### Contribution statement template
"In this paper, we [METHOD] using [DATA SOURCE] for [PERIOD] to [OBJECTIVE].
Our identification strategy exploits [VARIATION / INSTRUMENT].
We find that [KEY RESULT, post-study only].
This contributes to the literature by [SPECIFIC CONTRIBUTION]."

### PRE-STUDY placeholder for economics
[PLACEHOLDER: In this paper, we [e.g. estimate a gradient boosting model /
apply a LASSO regression / use a difference-in-differences design] using
[e.g. ENTSO-E hourly spot price data] for [e.g. 2015-2023] to examine
[RESEARCH QUESTION]. Our identifying variation comes from [VARIATION].
[KEY RESULTS: replace when available.]
This contributes to the literature on [STRAND] by [SPECIFIC CONTRIBUTION].]

### Roadmap conventions
Standard economics roadmap:
"Section 2 reviews the related literature. Section 3 describes the data and
empirical strategy. Section 4 presents the main results. Section 5 discusses
robustness checks. Section 6 concludes."

## Cohesion Techniques (Gray, 2018)

1. Repeated nouns or synonyms across sentences
   ("energy prices... price volatility... spot market dynamics...")
2. Transition words indicating logical relationships
   ("However" = contrast; "Therefore/Hence" = consequence;
   "Furthermore/Moreover" = addition; "Nevertheless" = concession)
3. Pronoun referents pointing back to prior sentences
   ("This volatility...", "These studies...", "Such approaches...")

Cohesion test: read only the first sentence of each paragraph.
Do they tell a coherent story? If yes, paragraph-level logic is sound.
