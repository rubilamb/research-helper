## Example Output (Introduction)

The rapid development of artificial intelligence has reshaped how
economic agents process information, allocate resources, and make
strategic decisions. A growing body of research has documented the
implications of AI adoption for productivity, labour markets, and
institutional governance (Qin et al., 2023; Capraro et al., 2023).
In particular, the emergence of large language models and autonomous
AI systems has introduced new dynamics into markets where algorithmic
agents interact with human decision-makers (Rothschild et al., 2025).
Studies have shown that AI-augmented firms achieve measurably different
outcomes in investment timing, risk assessment, and competitive
positioning (Csaszar et al., 2024).

However, a critical distinction has recently emerged between AI tools
that assist human decisions and agentic AI systems that act autonomously
on behalf of economic actors. Bohnsack and De Wet (2025) argue that
agentic AI represents a qualitative shift from decision support to
autonomous strategy execution. Despite growing attention to this
distinction, the economic implications of delegation to autonomous
agents remain poorly understood. Kobis et al. (2025) demonstrate that
delegation to AI can alter moral behaviour, while Sanabria and Vecino
(2024) show that market dynamics shift fundamentally when AI agents
transact on behalf of principals. Furthermore, evidence on whether
agentic AI improves or distorts economic efficiency is conflicting,
with some studies suggesting productivity gains (Ide and Talamas, 2023)
and others warning of systemic risks from correlated algorithmic
behaviour (Chan et al., 2023).

Despite this growing literature, no study has systematically examined
how the transition from assistive to agentic AI affects firm-level
economic decision-making across sectors. Existing work has focused on
specific applications (e.g., trading agents in finance or chatbots in
customer service) without addressing the broader structural implications
for economic organisation. The relationship between the degree of AI
autonomy and economic outcomes at the firm level remains largely
unexplored (Deng et al., 2024).

This paper examines the effect of agentic AI adoption on firm-level
decision quality and economic performance using a panel dataset of
publicly listed firms from 2020 to 2025. We exploit variation in the
timing of agentic AI deployment across firms and sectors to estimate
the causal effect of autonomous AI adoption on investment efficiency,
measured by Tobin's Q and return on assets. Our identification strategy
leverages staggered adoption patterns and controls for firm-level
confounders using a difference-in-differences framework with
heterogeneity-robust estimators (Callaway and Sant'Anna, 2021).

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows: Section 2 reviews the
related literature on AI and economic decision-making. Section 3
describes the data and empirical strategy. Section 4 presents the main
results. Section 5 discusses robustness checks. Section 6 concludes.

## Why This Example Works

**CARS structure (applied invisibly):**
- Move 1 (paragraphs 1-2): Establishes the territory of AI in economics,
  cites seminal and recent work, builds a narrative arc
- Move 2 (paragraph 3): Identifies the gap: no systematic study of
  agentic AI's effect on firm-level decisions across sectors
- Move 3 (paragraphs 4-5): States the research question, method, data,
  identification strategy, and roadmap

**Citation quality:**
- 10 citations total, all from the reference table
- Every paragraph has at least one citation
- Claims are attributed to specific papers, not generic assertions
- No fabricated references

**Anderson checklist score: 5/5**
1. Written pre-study (placeholder for results)
2. Explicit research question in Move 3
3. Gap statement in Move 2 predicts the study design
4. Linking conjunctions throughout (However, Despite, Furthermore)
5. All citations traceable to reference table

**What to notice:**
- No "Move 1", "Move 2", "Move 3" labels appear in the text
- The funnel narrows naturally from broad (AI in economics) to specific
  (agentic AI's effect on firm-level decisions)
- The gap is significant, not just "no one has studied X"
- The methodology is concrete (DID, specific data, specific period)
